You have **13 weeks until Sunday, October 18, 2026**—enough time to finish the Detroit half comfortably, but the plan needs to respect your [joint] and the fact that you have not been running consistently. The smartest goal is a **planned run/walk half marathon**, not trying to force yourself back into continuous running immediately. ([Detroit Free Press Marathon][1])

## The strategy

You will do:

* **Three run/walk sessions each week**
* **One main strength session**
* Swimming, cycling or walking for additional aerobic work
* Pickleball, but not as though it is a recovery workout—it is still impact and lateral movement
* A long run/walk that gradually reaches **10 miles**

Use a run/walk interval from the beginning rather than waiting until you are exhausted:

* Start with **2 minutes running/1 minute walking**
* Progress to **3:1 or 4:1** only when the [joint] is handling the current ratio
* Keep the running portions genuinely easy—full-sentence conversational pace

A deliberate walk break every few minutes will give you a much better chance of reaching mile 13 feeling functional rather than merely surviving.

## Your weekly schedule

### Monday — Easy run/walk

Begin at 25–30 minutes and gradually reach 40–45 minutes. This should be very easy, especially after Sunday's long session.

Finish with 10 minutes of calf raises, bridges, banded side steps and balance work.

### Tuesday — Pickleball

Keep it moderate. Avoid playing four hours or repeatedly chasing every difficult ball while your running volume is climbing.

### Wednesday — Strength and optional cardio

Strength train for 35–45 minutes:

* Deadlift or Romanian deadlift
* Glute bridge or hip thrust
* Controlled step-ups
* Hamstring curl
* Calf raises
* Row or pulldown
* Core work

Keep **two or three repetitions in reserve**. Once your long run reaches seven miles, this is not the time to chase another deadlift PR. Strong supporting muscles can help the [joint] absorb load, but strength work needs to support the running rather than compete with it. ([OrthoInfo][2])

An easy swim or 20–30 minutes of cycling can follow if you feel good.

### Thursday — Second run/walk

This progresses from 30 to approximately 45 minutes. Most weeks remain easy.

Beginning in Week 5, every other Thursday can include:

* 10 minutes easy
* Four to six rounds of 60–90 seconds on a gentle incline
* Easy walking or jogging between repetitions
* 10 minutes easy

For the International Half, controlled incline training will help prepare you for the Ambassador Bridge without needing hard speedwork. The currently published course description includes both the bridge and Detroit-Windsor Tunnel. ([Detroit Free Press Marathon][1])

### Friday — Rest or shortened pickleball

Two full pickleball sessions plus three runs creates **five impact days**. That may be too much for your [joint].

You can play Friday when the [joint] feels normal, but keep it shorter and easier. During Weeks 8–12, Friday should become a rest day whenever you are carrying soreness or fatigue.

### Saturday — Rest and mobility

Walk gently if desired, but arrive at Sunday's session with fresh legs.

### Sunday — Long run/walk

This is the most important workout of the week.

## Your [joint] rules

Before starting Week 1, you should be able to briskly walk for approximately 45 minutes without swelling, limping or clearly worse pain afterward.

During the plan:

* Mild awareness that does not change your stride may be manageable.
* Pain that makes you limp, swelling, instability or worsening pain the following morning means the load was too high.
* Replace the next run with swimming or easy cycling.
* Resume at the last level your [joint] handled successfully.

Return-to-running guidance commonly recommends walk/jog intervals after injury and stepping back when running creates pain, swelling or altered mechanics. ([Massachusetts General Hospital][3])

Because you previously had [joint] effusion, suspected meniscus involvement and pain on stairs, **recurrent swelling is not something to train through**. That should trigger a sports-medicine or PT reassessment.

## Fueling practice

Starting with the six-mile long run:

* Eat a familiar carbohydrate-based breakfast 90–150 minutes beforehand.
* Begin taking carbohydrates during the run rather than waiting until you feel depleted.
* Aim initially for roughly **30–45 grams of carbohydrate per hour** through gels, chews or a sports drink.
* Drink according to thirst and weather conditions.
* Use the long runs to determine what your stomach tolerates.

Endurance guidance commonly places carbohydrate intake in approximately the 30–60 grams-per-hour range for prolonged exercise. ([ACSM][4])

Do not combine half-marathon training with aggressive calorie restriction. You can still lose weight gradually, but underfueling the long runs will make recovery worse and increase the chance that the plan falls apart.

## Race-day execution

Start with the same run/walk interval you used during the ten-mile run.

For the first three miles, deliberately hold back. On the International course, power-walking part of the bridge is completely reasonable. After mile 9, you can lengthen the running intervals only if you still feel controlled.

The International Half has a four-hour last-chance pace, while the Motor City Half has a 3½-hour last-chance pace. Both are compatible with a trained run/walk approach, although the Motor City cutoff is tighter. ([Detroit Free Press Marathon][1])

Your real milestone is not running ten miles continuously. It is completing the Week 11 ten-miler with your planned walk breaks, manageable fatigue and **no [joint] swelling later that day or the next morning**. If you can do that, you will be positioned to cross the finish line comfortably.
