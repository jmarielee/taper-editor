# The Taper Editor — report on your July 2025 triathlon plan

I read your whole plan. Before I can finish, one question:

**ON HOLD — one question before the race-week check resolves.**
Your plan builds toward a race on July 12, but it never says what that race is *for*. **Is this race the goal, or training for something else?** Those are different plans — how hard you should be working in the final week depends entirely on the answer, so I can't rule on that part until you tell me.

Everything else I found stands on its own. Here it is, most important first.

---

## 1. Every single date in your plan is on the wrong day — including race day.

**Your plan says:** "Fri, July 12 — TRIATHLON DAY! — Race strong and enjoy what you've trained for."

**The problem:** *Calendar and arithmetic integrity* — every date's weekday has to match a real calendar, and every number has to add up. July 12, 2025 was a **Saturday**, not a Friday. And it isn't just race day: all twelve days in your plan's table are labeled one weekday off. July 1 is labeled "Mon" — it was a Tuesday. The whole table is shifted, and the plan even contradicts itself: its own opening line correctly says "Sprint triathlon on Saturday, July 12," then the table calls that day Friday.

**What it costs you:** You execute the calendar, not the intention. A plan whose days are all mislabeled can put your hardest workout, your rest day, or your gear prep on the wrong real day — and it puts "race day" on a day the race isn't happening. If you trusted the labels over the dates, you'd taper for Friday and race tired on Saturday.

**Your call:** Which do you follow — the dates or the day names? And what does it tell you about the rest of this plan that nothing in it caught this?

## 2. Ten of your twelve training days have no session length. Your final week can't be checked.

**Your plan says:** "Wed, July 3 — Recovery + Core — Light walk or yoga + 15–20 min core" — and that's one of the *more* specific days. Most read like "Tues, July 9 — Pickleball — Just pickleball today — keep pace moderate."

**The problem:** *Calendar and arithmetic integrity*, again — if a plan's training amounts can't be added up, that itself is a finding. An athlete executes numbers. Only two of twelve days state how long anything should take, so there is no way — for you or for me — to compute whether your final week actually winds down.

**What it costs you:** "Taper" means reducing your training in the last stretch before a race so you arrive rested. This plan *claims* to taper (see Finding 4), but with no durations, the claim can't be verified and you can't follow it precisely. "Keep pace moderate" is a mood, not a workout. On any given day you're deciding the actual dose yourself — which means the plan isn't really deciding anything.

**Your call:** Before you trust a plan like this again — should a plan that can't be added up be allowed to tell you it tapers?

## 3. Your plan's only safety limit is a checkbox with no consequences.

**Your plan says:** "☐ No extra workouts — follow the plan, not the mood"

**The problem:** *Missing ceiling* — every stated limit needs a defined response for when it's broken. This is the only line in the entire plan that limits how much you train, and it's an unchecked box on a daily checklist. The plan never says what happens if you add a workout anyway, and never mentions the limit again.

**What it costs you:** Self-coached athletes deviate by *adding*, not skipping. A limit with no response to being broken has silently decided the answer is "nothing" — and you find that out after the race, in your legs. A hollow safeguard is worse than a missing one, because it looks like protection.

**Your call:** Is this a rule with a response, or a wish?

## 4. The plan certifies its own safety.

**Your plan says:** "You're already well-trained — this plan focuses on sharpening and tapering, not overtraining."

**The problem:** *Self-certification* — a plan's claims about its own quality are claims, not facts. This sentence reads like a review already happened. It didn't. And per Finding 2, the taper claim isn't even checkable from the plan's own numbers — there aren't any.

**What it costs you:** Confident self-assessment is the house style of AI-generated plans. If you read that sentence and relaxed, the sentence did its job — reassurance — without doing the work — verification. You went into race week believing a check had been done that no one performed.

**Your call:** Does the math support the claim? (It can't — there is no math.)

## 5. The plan has no rule for a bad day — in either direction.

**Your plan says:** "☐ Today's workout done (swim / bike / run / lift / pickleball / recovery)"

**The problem:** *No bad-day rule* — a plan needs defined behavior for the two ways real training diverges from paper: the missed session and the added one. This plan defines only compliance — check the box. It never says what a skipped day changes, or what an extra one costs.

**What it costs you:** A plan that accommodates everything protects against nothing. When life happened, this plan just re-formed around whatever you did — so it was never actually steering. You were.

**Your call:** When you miss a day or add one, who decides what changes — the plan, or the mood?
